On Onomastics: A Current State of the Search on my Name

Posted at — Dec 15, 2022

Townie Shit

  I come from a place called Highland Lakes in the township of Vernon, sitting just south on NJ’s northern border with NY. Highland Lakes is very aptly named, it’s a community situated on a bunch of lakes on a mountain, the mountain itself holding the far older name of Wawayanda. These ridges and valleys make up our part of the East Coast’s premiere mountain chain: the Appalachians. Up here, “Appalachian” rhymes with “nation.” Vernon itself is quite old by US standards, having been established not even a couple decades after the revolution back in 1793. The origins of its name are unknown, but it’s been speculated that it could be after the English Admiral Edward Vernon, who served with Washington’s brother and to which Washington’s residence in Virginia also owes its name.

  Legally, I’ve never left Vernon despite having lived away for over 6 years (due to my undergraduate and master’s education). It’s a curious place in the NY Metro area, as despite its proximity to the city it represents a pretty rural life. Neither the trains nor the buses come here, even though it’s situated just an hour from Manhattan. In fact, it is the most populated town in this county, only making weirder its lack of connection to anything else.

  The people here will often say that they “live in the woods.” While some of the neighborhoods themselves can be dense enough that it might not look too different from a typical suburban neighborhood (at least typical for the sidewalk-less kind), Highland Lakes is engulfed in thick forests protected under Wawayanda State Park that encroach right up to people’s backyards. The Appalachian Trail passes through those woods, giving locals the chance to meet with friendly international trespassers on their properties.

  Living within protected forests lends the community not only to meeting diverse trail-trekkers, but also to having to share their land with the other locals: the wildlife. In my backyard I used to meet all kinds of critters, one of my favorites being the perpetually globular-looking groundhogs who jiggle when they run (at shockingly high speeds) and one of whom we affectionately named “Stanley Häagen-Dazs” for our suspicions on his diet. And while here you will find more deer than anywhere, any local will tell you that the real stars here are the bears. This leads to such fun headlines as:

Black bear knocks down Vernon man, steals sandwich

  After a population lowpoint of less than 25 bears in the state of NJ in 1971, bear hunts were closed.1 However, by the time I was born in 98 their population had skyrocketed to such an extent that it was not uncommon to see bears monthly. Even cuter with their cubs! We were even taught in school how to act around bears, and I myself have almost walked into a mom with her cubs eating trash on my way home from school. These neighbors polarize the community, as many of the locals would like to hunt them, both for sport and for protection. Meanwhile, many citizens wish to protect them further as they too are part of the community, and we are living on their land. We even have activists for the cause.

Petition to pardon Vernon bear activist’s jail sentence signed by more than 83,000 people

  This issue is further exascerbated in that many of the residents who hold these more protective stances also tend to not be from the area, but rather from “the city” or its actual suburbs and satellite cities on the Jersey-side of the Hudson. This region, referred to by NJ’s board of tourism as “the Skylands” (though locals normally prefer to identify culturally with the county, Sussex) is as close to the city as you can get if you want a mountain oasis, and the town knows it. Here we have recreational activities for all 4 seasons including skiing, snowdoarding, golfing, mountain biking, swimming, windsurfing, etc. Mountain Creek, a ski resort/waterpark/bike park/employer of most of the townie teenagers is one of the institutions that puts Vernon on the map. Formerly known as Action Park, if there’s anything out-of-towners might be aware of from here it is the lack of foresight and supervision that existed in the park, most famously with the case of the world’s first looping waterslide which lasted 2 months before closing. There’s even a movie based on the park called Action Point. Its ski resort, while perhaps less famous, is of higher-esteem, famously being where Shaun White qualified for the winter olympics.

  The town doesn’t just have resorts and amenities, but many of the communities are almost setup for that purpose fulltime. Here you find the concept of a “lake community,” which is a “thing” (enough of “a thing” that I recognized Bon Appetit’s Brad Leone as being one of us after him saying in a video that he’s “from a lake community”).

“Lake Community” Representation. There’s a “clubhouse” and everything!

  Didn’t take too long before I found headlines like this, confirming my suspicions:

Sussex County Chef Talks Action Park Fatalities While Eating Crazy Spicy Wings On ‘Hot Ones’

  These communities, as the name implies, are situated on lakes which are often artificially made. They were built relatively recently, but back in the pre-air conditioner days. In the case of Highland Lakes, it was built back starting in 1933, one of many such communities across the region designed to attract New Yorkers to the area as a getaway. To this day, many of the residents in the summer are New Yorkers and other wealthy out-of-towners who own a vacation home, providing people like my parents a job in keeping their residences clean. It is due to people like them that I was able to experience some of the amenities of my town, like the spa we have (Minerals) which I used to get free passes for. While they bring money in, it also was quite noticeable how the population changed over the summer, especially when working at the beaches. I myself was a lifeguard, and most of my coworkers were from these types of families, and many of the people I was guarding weren’t from here either. I learned to always be watchful of children with names that rhyme with “Aiden,” a name that I never encountered from townies but yet seemed to exist in droves in the summerhomeowner class. I’ve yet to meet one that survived passed the age of 13.

  Given the recentness of these communities’ existences, many of the townies themselves are born to parents from closer to the city or even immigrants who came via the city. These people ended up here partly due to its cheapness compared to the city, as well as the appeal of the activities and the nature. My father chose to live here because he loves the mountains, so this was his closest option to NY and was more affordable. Vernon itself had nearly 16 times the population in 2000 as it did in 1950, so by far most people have moved in recently despite the 2 centuries of history since the town was established.

  So, with all these newcomers… what are their origins?

  I remember talking to a friend in Europe about the diversity in the US and they said something like “but still most people are English no? They probably just don’t say so” which made me think… I knew exactly one person who identified as English… because his dad was an immigrant from England. I did know people with English last names who’d call themselves Italian or Irish or something, so maybe most people just identify with most-recent-immigrant? There is a way I could do a quick sample from Vernon at least, and that’s just by going to see the honor roll list which my old high school used to publish. And surely enough:

  Italian, Polish, Italian, German, English, Polish, German, German, Hungarian, Spanish, Swedish, etc.

  Definitely a small minority with English names, and no clear predominance. The funny thing is that this area is known for its lack of diversity. By American standards it’s true, it’s an incredibly white area and there is a past to that (we can take a guess at who were the wealthy elites that were being attracted to live in these lake communities). Plus, in the US there is a tendency to assimilate. In the past I feel like this was somewhat obligatory for survival, but in today’s day and age I think it’s also really hard “not to” just because of how pervasive America’s cultural influence is. However, these cultural influences definitely leave a mark, at the very least if a friend said they were Italian I could at least rely on enjoying the food at their home when I came to visit them. 😂

  With this assimilation though, it is not uncommon for someone to not only not know what their name means, but also to have no idea where it comes from. It’s a curious thing, because it’s something that tells such a history. Also, at least in my case I don’t have any heirlooms and don’t inherit much, so my name is probably the most ancient thing I have. And perhaps it goes a bit deeper in American culture. Maybe with so many different names, nobody cares? I’ve rarely ever been asked about my name, and when I see an interesting name admittedly I don’t have much of an instinct to ask about it. And it’s not just names of people, I feel like it’s really with everything.

  Back in my neighborhood, nearly all of the roads have indigenous-sounding names. Never once have I heard anyone question what they mean or what they refer to. In fact, I’m willing to bet almost everyone doesn’t know what language they’re in. I can say that with certainty because I cannot find what language they’re in, and I have been trying to contact the local government and historical society about it, but I haven’t been answered yet. Furthermore, I’m pretty sure most people don’t know what language was even spoken here before colonization. It’s Munsee, a subtripe of the Lenape, whose language has also given us names like Manhattan and Weehawken. There are only 2 native speakers left, both of whom live in Canada.

  There is one name that almost surely comes from Munsee here, and that is of the mountain itself, Wawayanda. Technically the mountain’s name come from a lake on the mountain. Even then, it’s actual meaning is uncertain, mostly because two people who claim to have met with members of the tribe gave two completely different explanations: one being that it means “egg-shaped,” the other being that it means “the way of the wild goose.”

Luckily for them, usually it’s not that hard to google it, and even when it’s not google-able there’s a good chance the name is spelt in such a way that someone from that culture would recognize it as “probably one of theirs,” so the person might be able to just ask around. But, this doesn’t work for everyone…

  And it doesn’t work for me.

  Despite knowing exactly where my ancestors are from, it occurred to me pretty early on that my name is weirdly unique. Gaura.

  It’s easy to forget that this place has a history.
