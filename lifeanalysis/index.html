Life Analysis

Posted at — Dec 02, 2022

My goals in each part of my life:

High school

In high school I just wanted to get into a good school. I knew I was smart and capable, and I figured I could sort everything out when I’m older. Plus, I have a lot of interests, so it made sense to me to see how intensely I could study everything. Of course, I did gravitate to certain things in particular that took up my time and that I was most interested in:

  • Anything music (orchestra, chamber, pit, choir, chanticleer, etc.)
  • Church (altar serving, Antioch, mission trip, etc.) and community service (key club)
  • Math

Funnily enough, thinking back on it, I really was a music guy. Like, definitively. I did so much with music, and my favorite class (as well as my most challenging class) was AP Music Theory. On the other hand, math came easy to me, and math class was normally on the positive side. Always comfortable. Plus, I would get a lot of gratification from solving hard problems, and I actually found working on problems to be kind of “meditative.”

When it came to thinking about what I would wanna major in, I remember putting 3 majors:

  • Math
  • Computer Science
  • Music

I think on some forms I might’ve also written neuroscience or something like that. Math felt like such a safe option, with so many prospects. Computer science even moreso prospect-wise… but I had no idea whether I’d like it, but “in theory” I bet I would (I was more than right). And music, well, let’s be real. I was/am a violinist, and was nothing close to professional. I still struggle with just having a nice clear sound, so the competition would’ve been insane for me. Plus, I wasn’t that interested in perfecting my sound, I just liked the feeling of performing. As long as I could do that, it would be enough. I can literally do that at home. So, turning it into a hobby didn’t sound horrible at all, it even sounded nearly equivalent. That being said, I did want to go over just why I liked it so much:

  • It’s meditative to lose yourself in the music
  • Just like when solving a problem, it’s gratifying to perform something “correctly” i.e. as you imagined it. That takes effort and it’s gratifying!
  • There’s something about the communal spirit of being in-tune with those around you and focusing on melding with your surroundings
  • There is a bit of a rush of performing in front of people, it’s not much as I had gotten quite used to it, but I was a fan of the feeling :)

University

So hurray, I got accepted to my top school on a full-ride for math. What now? Well, I was definitely gonna continue with my math major, but I had some other goals in mind:

  • Definitely experiment with a ton of interesting classes, especially sense stuff!
  • Learn computer science whether-or-not you’ll switch majors for it
  • Quantum stuff!!! :ooo
  • Also do a study abroad at some point… and really take any opportunity you can to travel
  • Maybe learn a new language?? :ooo
  • Get fit??
  • Eat healthier??
  • Graduate with a math degree!!

It’s funny to think about these being my priorities… because it didn’t even occur to me that I succeeded so much. In fact, along the lines of “experimenting with classes” and traveling, I even took evening classes in perfumery in New York in my senior year! Damn I’ve done a lot. I also got to take really cool “sense” classes in color and sound as well! The computer science stuff has also been more than successful, with my thesis even being a computer science thesis. The quantum stuff was just like a stint I had, but I was very satisfied with what I learned and got over wanting to pursue anything in it (there just straight up aren’t that many useful algorithms that utilize quantum properties, so the real work is more in engineering than software).

It’s wild to think about now, but up until that point I had barely traveled at all. Since my freshman year, I’ve now lived, worked, and/or studied in the US, Japan, China, Russia, Finland, France, Poland, Norway, and Belgium. Isn’t that insane?? And I’d readily do more XD!

I quit every time I tried to learn a new language, but it wasn’t a big priority at the time. And who cares? I’ve completed that since then anyways XD. I was also able to take classes in linguistics so I got a bit of my fix.

Getting fit and eating healthier has definitely been a weaker spot. However, I was considered fit by the one time I went to a doctor there lol, and I did eat diversely sometimes, like I would eat my veggies on occasion especially towards my senior year, so it was getting there.

It definitely seems worth noting that my only goal with math was to get the degree… I didn’t know what I’d do with it, or even what in math I wanted to learn. I just wanted the experience (and I was hoping it would be clearer what my options were over time). Those options did not become clearer, I had trouble maintaining my math interest to know what fields I wanted to explore, etc. However, I do think those courses were really important for my development, as there is a type of thinking that these classes require that I think has since become very beneficial to me (breaking things up into the bare rules that comprise them and see what those imply, etc.). On top of that, it makes reading and digesting anything technical a lot easier for me, so I don’t shy away from it in my computer science stuff nowadays, and it encourages me to explore fields like category theory.

I also had some personality goals, like:

  • Be less picky
  • Be more courageous

I’m actually shocked how successful “be less picky” was. All my life that has been such a huge struggle, and it literally just disappeared after going to Japan. And with the courage, technically it’s still being worked on, but I know that what I meant at the time was the ability to do things on my own. I knew I could, but there was always this invisible barrier being like “you’re a baby you’re so young you don’t know anything you can’t handle this etc. etc.” This would apply to things like buying train tickets, going on buses, etc. WILD how different my attitude is on doing random bureacratic bullshit and going about weird tasks in my life now. However, I still have some random small problems, but they’re small enough that they’re not the biggest concern of mine.

In any case, besides all those goals, what was I doing outside of class?

  • Sinfonia
  • Matriculate
  • Teaching internships (and a research internship)

Clearly, again, I gravitated towards a particular thing: teaching. And I loved it! Some pros and cons:

  • Love being able to give live feedback and interaction
  • Seeing someone grow is very special and emotionally gratifying
  • However, teachinng almost feels like you are putting yourself on hold to help someone else

My main fear with pursuing some kind of path in education is that I wouldn’t have time to develop myself. I feel like many jobs I’d be interested in would have some component of constant personal development, and while I guess character and effectiveness-wise that could be true for teaching, I don’t think that’d develop myself as much as I’d like. Again, I can make further development of my interests my hobbies… but I think it’d be nice to have those interests more represented in my work. So, it was starting to feel like a future as a professor would be best of both worlds… so maybe graduate school is the answer? Some pros and cons:

  • Get to have more experiences in a somewhat chill environment
  • Prolongs my indecision about what to do about the future a bit longer
  • But kind of pts my life on hold care career-development-wise
  • If I go for a PhD… that could be a looot of work

Judging how I work during theses, the PhD sounded quite scary. I’d need to really dedicate myself to one. single. topic. But I have so many interests! What if I get distracted? (seriously)

However, it made a lot of sense for graduate school in computer science, especially since I found a program in sense stuff! And it’d make it a lot easier to say that I did indeed have valuable computer science skills instead of just “took a couple classes.”

Graduate school

New environment, new goals. Here goes:

  • Learn to cook [better]
  • Get fit??
  • Touch more grass
  • EXPLOREEE
  • Learn as much as you can on colors, graphics, and perception stuff. Extra plus if for web
  • Learn a language
  • Figure out what you wanna do in life
  • Figure out where you’d prefer to live

Eh, you kinda learned how to cook, but definitely could’ve done better. Luckily, I know why I’ve had issues, and I think those would be resolved if I just stably live somewhere longer term. I also think I’m technically more fit, but still, I’m not putting in the real work yet.

As for touching grass, I’m also proud of this one! I’ve made a bunch of new friends from all around the world and I feel like I fit in just fine :). I have had to make some adjustments with alcohol culture and some of my own obnoxiousness, but that’s part of growing older and getting more experienced.

Deeeefinitely took advantage of exploring. Holy shit have I traveled. And I could’ve done more if I really wanted! But I’m satisfied :).

With the colors, again, above and beyond what I imagined. I will say, however, virtual reality stuff was a disappointment, and I was hoping I’d learn Unity or something but it didn’t quite happen like that. And that’s no issue because that wasn’t my focus, but it is a bit of a condemnation on this program.

Lol when I wanted to learn a language I originally meant that I wanted to learn Japanese… but instead I learned Spanish! Funny how things work out, and maybe we’ll get around to Japanese at some point, we’ll see.

As for figuring out what to do and where to live… still a big question mark. That’s why I’m writing this now. I will say, I do think I can live kinda anywhere, but the environment matters a lot for me. I think if I could, I’d prefer to live somewhere:

  • Urban
  • With accessible mountains (or similarly interesting geography)
  • Speaks a language that I’d care to learn

On the job stuff, I originally thoguht I was going to continue onto a PhD. And lol. lmao. We know how that went. But it’s a good thing, because this came with it’s pros and cons as well:

  • In Ghent (awesome)
  • Study colors!!
  • Be on the path to a future in being an expert in colors :)
  • Prestige :oooo (meh, but like, could be a plus in the future)
  • Need to learn Dutch?
  • Work for a supervisor described as racist, sexist, etc. who overworks his students and makes them cry (and who you were warned about by the current student you’d be working with)
  • Work under an administration that doesn’t seem to care about you (and turned out, quite literally didn’t)
  • Figure out the freaking housing in that competitive-ass city
  • Have to publish something basically yearly and work super hard
  • Get paid $2,000 a month!! >:(

The cons were already sounding like they outweighed the pros before I even found out I didn’t really have the position. So good riddance. Problem is that I think it was my master’s thesis which prevented me from having the PhD, so if I wanna continue that path I’ll probably have to do extra work to revise it and make a better thing that I can actually get published-published, apply to a bunch of stuff in the future, etc. but then I’d have to put up with this kind of bullshit again. It’s not unique to this program, and they never pay well. It seems it would be better that I start to dedicate myself to finding a job, I think this path is temporarily closed.

(that being said, it might be something you’d be interested in doing in, like, 10 years or so. You can try to do what Ruth did! Something part-time might be a good idea cause it still suits your interests to do a PhD, I think it’d be valuable just to myself to have the title… but again it’s not THAT valuable, it’d just be neat)

So, what’s made me happy?

So we got:

  • Music and performance
  • Teaching and working with people
  • Reading research on anything related to senses, linguistics, etc. etc. anything related to “representations”
  • Doing my website!
