Introduction

   One of those “interesting facts” I hear tossed around is that colors like, purple, pink, and magenta are not real colors. Of course, what they mean is that you can’t find them on the rainbow… and that’s true for pink and magenta. But I have also heard that purple and violet are not really on the rainbow. After all, we have cones for “red,” “green,” and “blue,” therefore anything purple has to be a mix of red and blue without green, which is impossible on the rainbow because they’re at opposite ends. So, really, we’re just imagining that violet/purple is there.

Science

   Luckily for us, color science is indeed a real thing and this issue should be quite easy to solve. In fact, the core of colorimetry (the measurement of color) lies in the color matching functions. In particular, we can define all colors based on mixtures of three others, originally using the colors produced by light of 435.8 nm, 546.1 nm, and 700 nm. With a little matrix transformation, we can represent these colors in a standard space called XYZ. Most notably, we can also easily derive displayable RGB colors we know and love from XYZ. So, let’s display the rainbow! In fact, we got a source for the data right here.

   Okay we’re not quite there yet, we have one major issue: we literally can’t display these rainbow colors. Our monitors are made up of only three lights, which together define a subset of colors which are displayable.

Discussion

   I’d highly recommend the reader to try every iteration of minimizing and maximizing the first three sliders after hitting the ▶️ button, the results look very neat! What can be concluded is that the idea works very well, and it also shows the influence each dimension has. It seems that saturation is by far the least important/information-giving of the dimensions, followed by hue, followed by luminance, which is by far the most important dimension. It’s also interesting to see how saturation appears when there is no hue or luminance.
