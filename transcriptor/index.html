Background

  Back in high school, I wanted to teach some friends some Polish words and phrases. Since sometimes I was doing this over text (in English, in the US), I needed a way to write Polish sounds into English so they could read it. This isn’t too hard as I wasn’t trying to get them to anunciate perfectly, I just hoped they could get close enough, and the English interpretation of the alphabet is good enough for doing that.

  Well, almost.

  The more words and phrases I tried to transcribe, the more I realized “oh wait, there’s multiple ways they might read this.” For example, there’s a town in Poland called Ełk. The Ł makes a W-sound in Polish… so clearly it should be spelt “Ewk” in an English transcription… but then someone might read it as “Yuke” or “Ew-k” (😖). So maybe I can transcribe it as “Ehwk”… but will they understand that the point of the H is just to keep the E sounding like a regular E (/ɛ/)? Problems, problems, problems…

  I had only just learned to read Polish at the time, which is partly why I was so interested in sharing the language with my friends. The experience was surprisingly fun! I had already known the language as a heritage speaker, and Polish uses mostly the same alphabet as English, it just interprets some of the letters differently and has a couple extra letters. One day I got a Polish Harry Potter book, and as I forced myself to read it I realized I was able to figure out the oddities of the alphabet on my own, and quickly! In fact, I didn’t need to look anything up. This was a bit crazy, because to this day when I encounter a new English word as a native speaker I might need to look up the pronunciation, but when reading Polish I was able to:

  • Figure out the sounds of all new letters (e.g. Ą, Ć, Ę, etc.)
  • Figure out the sounds of certain combinations of letters (digraphs & trigraphs) that weren’t obvious (e.g. Ci, Ch, Cz, Dz, Dzi, Dź, etc.)
  • Figure out unique Polish phonology rules (e.g. devoicing, contextual pronunciations of Ą & Ę, etc.)
  • Know what every written word is supposed to sound like

   As my first major experience with a non-English alphabet, it was insane to me how easy reading could be. What if English could be like that?

Introduction

  To those who aren’t native to English, especially those who speak languages with alphabets or decent Romanizations of their scripts, it probably comes as no surprise that English spelling leaves a lot to be desired when it comes to reflecting the actual pronunciation of the word. In fact, often it fails miserably by that metric! Why does one = wun? How does read ≠ read? This is particularly well shown by the poem “The Chaos” by Gerard Nolst Trenité:

Dearest creature in creation
Studying English pronunciation,
I will teach you in my verse
Sounds like corpse, corps, horse and worse.
 
I will keep you, Susy, busy,
Make your head with heat grow dizzy;
Tear in eye, your dress you'll tear;
Queer, fair seer, hear my prayer.
(I also recommend this poem as an exercize for students learning English)

  Now, many years ago, I tried to create my own writing system to address these problems. It turned out to be a much harder task than I realized! This would lead me down rabbitholes of phonology and historical linguistics, researching writing systems to the extent that I consider this work as a hobby of mine, and there is still much to learn. Worst of all, there is no perfect system… but we can try! So, on this page I offer one of my latest attempts at a revised English alphabet. This revised system is made with the following goals in mind:

  • Letters may be added to accomadate “poorly”-represented sounds
    e.g. TH represents both /θ/ (like bath) and /ð/ (like bathe), so those two sounds are represented poorly as there is no distinction when written)
    • Letters, whenever possible, should be taken from as closely related a language as possible (i.e. Old English, then other Germanic languages, and when that fails, other Indo-European languages)
    • Digraphs (combinations of two letters, like SH and TH) are acceptable
  • Letters may change sound depending on context
    e.g. the A in stat is not the same sound as in state, this is an acceptable rule
  • Pronunciation rules should match, as closely as possible, to pre-existing intuitions that an English speaker would have when reading
    e.g. most letters should make the same sound they already do
  • The writing should be “pretty”!
    • Try to minimize use of accents and other diacritics
    • Try to minimize the size of words (but prioritize diacritic minimization)
  • The writing should be able to be used by most English speakers, or at least by most North Americans, with minimal variations in spelling to account for regionalisms
  • The writing should reflect English pronunciation phenomena that are not obvious

  It is that last point that is particularly hard to get down, but also particularly satisfying when it works well! Below I describe some of the phenomena I struggled with and how I accomadated them.

What to do About English Weirdness

1. Diphthongs and Long Vowels… huh?

Preface

[PL] hejnał ≈ [EN] hay now

  One of the few memories I have that remain from kindergarten is getting taught the alphabet and the different sounds the letters make. I remember my teacher explaining the difference between long vowels and short vowels and thinking, basically, this makes no sense. The long vowels do seem longer than the short ones, yes1, but they make completely different sounds.

short A = A as in stat
long A = A as in state

  …huh? You’d think that a long A would literally just be the short A sound… but longer. In many languages (e.g. Hungarian2), that is indeed how their long and short vowel distinction works! What’s going on?

O + W = OW as in cow

  …again, what?? O never makes an A-sound like in stat, yet when combined with W it does! Why isn’t it just written with an AW then? (but then AW already exists and makes its own sound…)

  When creating my first writing systems, the temptation was to do away with this intuition and declare that “long vowels are fake and an outdated concept.” Same deal with the diphthongs (vowel combinations like OW). Wouldn’t it just be easier to represent them as the sum of their parts? Long A sounds like E as in dress (i.e. a short E) with a Y-sound [approximately] coming after it, so why not just write it as EY? This is basically what Polish does! Polish J makes the English Y-sound, and Ł makes the English W-sound, therefore you get the words hejnał and hay now3 sounding very similar. Maybe, instead, English can write hey naw.

The Problem with Canadian Raising

  A math textbook here might invite the reader to try to write all diphthong-sounding vowels as combinations like long A should be written as EY, OW should be written as AW, etc. For me, a couple issues came up:

  1. Words start to get pretty big
  2. … something is up with “long I”

  (1) is only a problem because in previous writing system-attempts the text looked pretty clunky, which is why I made it a goal to minimize word length in the first place. The issue with (2) is as follows:

tight ≉ tide
rice ≉ rise
strife ≉ strive
etc.

  In theory, the vowel should sound the same, no?

  There are actually two phenomena happening here (for me). For most native English speakers, vowels before devoiced consonants (like /t/, /s/, /f/, etc.) are shorter (in a literal way, not in a vowel-changing way) than those before voiced consonants (like /d/, /z/, /v/, etc.). Therefore, tight is normally said faster than tide. However, there is also a phenomenon called Canadian raising. This is when certain vowel sounds raise (i.e. change into other vowel sounds) in certain contexts. In particular, this affects /aʊ/ (OW as in cow) and /aɪ/ (the long I sound). That’s why Canadians have that stereotypical interesting-sounding pronunciations of words like about. However, while the /aʊ/-sound being affected is mostly a Canadian thing, the /aɪ/ being affected happens all over North America.

  For me, /aɪ/ sounds more like [ɑɪ] in tide, and sounds like [ʌɪ] in tight4. But, these are clearly allophones (different ways of saying the same sound as understood to listeners of the language), so it doesn’t make sense to differentiate these two cases even though I can hear the difference. Functionally, they’re the same. In fact, Canadian raising isn’t the only such phenomenon that is affecting vowels, there are a ton of such phenomena that affect various dialects of English.

  Solution: We cannot seperate out diphthongs. Each diphthong should be considered as its own vowel, even if there are allophones which are recognizably different. Therefore, my writing system will try to prescribe a unique vowel to all monophthongs and diphthongs.

Some new rules:

  • I makes A, E, and I long when they are stressed
  • Y makes A, E, and I long when they are unstressed
  • I and U are always stressed/accompany a stressed vowel, otherwise any similar sound is written as Ø
  • Vowels turn into their long counterparts when preceding another vowel (besides I and U) and at the end of words
  • J often = Y, J comes after consonants, Y comes after vowels, both can start words depending on how they treat previous words
  • U = W before stressed vowels
  • Ø is not written when obvious
  • Ø is written as O after the first stressed syllable has occurred (to assist with reading), except at the end of words, before R, or if it has secondary stress
  • A and E are either stressed or have secondary stress

There is something to be said about a truly disastrous meal, a meal forever indelible in your memory because it’s so uniquely bad, it can only be deemed an achievement. The sort of meal where everyone involved was definitely trying to do something; it’s just not entirely clear what.

I’m not talking about a meal that’s poorly cooked, or a server who might be planning your murder — that sort of thing happens in the fat lump of the bell curve of bad. Instead, I’m talking about the long tail stuff — the sort of meals that make you feel as though the fabric of reality is unraveling. The ones that cause you to reassess the fundamentals of capitalism, and whether or not you’re living in a simulation in which someone failed to properly program this particular restaurant. The ones where you just know somebody’s going to lift a metal dome off a tray and reveal a single blue or red pill.

I’m talking about those meals.

At some point, the only way to regard that sort of experience — without going mad — is as some sort of community improv theater. You sit in the audience, shouting suggestions like, “A restaurant!” and “Eating something that resembles food” and “The exchange of money for goods, and in this instance the goods are a goddamn meal!” All of these suggestion go completely ignored.
